In this module, we will research the history of computer science. We will analyze many interesting questions about computer history. For each question, we will discuss: when did it happen? Who played their part? what happened? And why is this important in the history of computer science?

1801

1. What does a loom have to do with Computer Science History?

In 1801, Frenchman Joseph Marie Jacquard demonstrated an easier way to create patterns for fabric through the invention of the loom. They created multiple punched cards with holes that told pins how they should be raised and pulled. Through the pulling of cords, the fabric wrapped around itself, creating an automated pattern. The loom made us realize the analogy of creating a sequence of operations to create or control something different. This is significant for computer science history because it inspired Charles Baggage to use a similar punch program in his Analytical Engine (mechanical computer) to create more than mathematical tables.

1821

2. Did the Difference Engine ever work?

In 1821, Charles Baggage thought of the idea of the difference engine to help create calculations faster and easier. He did build small prototypes of the engine. Someone would register their data, and then a single operation would take place and produce a solution. It had a memory for data to be processed later. It would print its output on soft metal to produce a printing plate. Likewise, it is important for computer science history because, after this, he had a better idea of the foundations of the difference engine. A machine to create more than one calibration at a time with unprecedented accuracy, the analytical engine.

1880

4. What was the Hollerith Machine

In 1880, the census took seven years to count, and everyone was fed up with taking the time. So, Herman Hollerith, who worked for the 1880 census, invented a punch card tally machine. Installed in federal government offices in 1888. Where people would punch in their data. Then, tabulation started reading the holes in each section. Thereafter, the clerk transcribed the data in his hands and put it in a designated sorter drawer. They would process 80 punch cards per minute. In 1890, the census was completed in two weeks. This is significant for computer science history because it brought in a new age of automatic data processing. In trying to analyze the data, it took months, then weeks, and now maybe seconds.

1944

5. What is the Stored Program?

In 1944, John von Neumann initiated the idea that a computer would store instructions in its memory and, when enabled, do a variety of tasks. The information will go in and be translated into 1s and 0s, or binary code, which sends electronic signals on how to represent or store the data. It goes through all the 1s and 0s until you find the information you need. This is significant for computer science history because it makes it possible for hardware and software to talk to each other to manage assets. It is the building block for computers, phones, and any device you would like to hear, watch, and read from.

6. Why did transitors replace the vaccum tubes

In 1947, transistors were invented by Lucent Technologies. Transistors amplify a signal, thus replacing vacuum tubes and ending the Golden Age of Radio. It is like an electronic switch in a computer that heats less and is smaller. The electronic switch had many circuits that could tell which area was active. This is important for computer science history because it is smaller than a postage stamp. Over time, they became minicomputers and started replacing mainframes. It was first seen in 1954 in The First Silicon Transistor from Texas Instruments. That establishing of a new era in 1956 where computers take less space.

7. What computer is patented as the first computer?

In 1947, J. Presper Eckert and John W. Mauchly filed a patent for the ENIAC, a general-purpose automatic computer, as the first computer. It was invalidated for a couple of reasons, but most importantly because John Atanasoff was said to have built the first automatic electronic digital computer. In 1939, John Atanasoff built a small prototype that could store data and perform addition and subtraction, called an automatic electronic digital computer. While the ENIAC was the first programmed computer, once it was given instructions, it could compute artillery range tables and calculations for the construction of a hydrogen bomb. This is important for computer science history because it gave recognition to John Atanasoff-Berry for building his computer earlier on, and it is a device in the public domain for everyone to use.

9. What did UoU (University of Utah) have to do with the history of the internet?

In 1957, the U.S. government had a fear of falling behind, which created NASA and DARPA. To develop technologies like rockets, weapons, and computers. led to the idea of building a “Galactic Network,” a way for everyone to stay in communication. Universities joined ARPANET to make this idea a reality. This led to communication between UCLA and Stanford, and UoU was the fourth new community and node. This is important for computer science history because they started building a network, so civilians could use it for everyday use, and they built the internet.

10. Was hypertext ever used before the internet?

In 1965, we coined the word hypertext. In 1968, it was Douglas Engelart who took the stage to show how significant hypertext was for jumping from one document to another for presentation. Hypertext is a string used to connect one document to another, searching for a word or phrase. Hypertext was stored in Hypercard, where it was stored information from anything from images, text, and tables. This is essential for computer science history because, on the Macintosh, if you are trying to do research on any kind of subject, the subject will pull up books, magazines, and links in one go. It was hypertext that would take a step further in building the internet.
