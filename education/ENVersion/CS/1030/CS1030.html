1801

1. What does a loom have to do with Computer Science History?

Through the development of the loom, Frenchman Joseph Marie Jacquard discovered a simpler method for making fabric designs in 1801. They made numerous cards with holes punched into them that instructed pins on how to be lifted and pulled. The fabric formed an automated pattern by wrapping around itself as the cables were pulled. We came to understand the concept of putting together a series of actions in order to produce or manage something different thanks to the loom. Because of this Charles Baggage used a similar punch program in his Analytical Engine (mechanical computer) to produce more than just mathematical tables, this is crucial for the history of computer science.

To make a printing plate, it would print its result on soft metal. Additionally, it is significant for the history of computer science since after this, he had a clearer understanding of the principles underlying the difference engine. 1944

5. What is the Stored Program?

In 1944, John von Neumann initiated the idea that a computer would store instructions in its memory and, when enabled, do a variety of tasks. The information will go in and be translated into 1s and 0s, or binary code, which sends electronic signals on how to represent or store the data. It goes through all the 1s and 0s until you find the information you need. This is significant for computer science history because it makes it possible for hardware and software to talk to each other to manage assets. It is the building block for computers, phones, and any device you would like to hear, watch, and read from.

7. What computer is patented as the first computer?

In 1947, J. Presper Eckert and John W. Mauchly filed a patent for the ENIAC, a general-purpose automatic computer, as the first computer. It was invalidated for a couple of reasons, but most importantly because John Atanasoff was said to have built the first automatic electronic digital computer. In 1939, John Atanasoff built a small prototype that could store data and perform addition and subtraction, called an automatic electronic digital computer. While the ENIAC was the first programmed computer, once it was given instructions, it could compute artillery range tables and calculations for the construction of a hydrogen bomb. This is important for computer science history because it gave recognition to John Atanasoff-Berry for building his computer earlier on, and it is a device in the public domain for everyone to use.

9. What did UoU (University of Utah) have to do with the history of the internet?

NASA and DARPA were established in 1957 as a result of the American government's concern over falling behind. To create technologies like as computers, weaponry, and rockets. Led to the notion of creating a "Galactic Network," a means of keeping everyone in contact. To make this concept a reality, universities joined ARPANET. UCLA and Stanford were able to communicate as a result, and UoU became the fourth new node in this new community. This is significant in the history of computer science because they began creating a network that people could use on a daily basis and consequently created the internet.

Hypertext is a string used to connect one document to another, searching for a word or phrase. Hypertext was stored in Hypercard, where it was stored information from anything from images, text, and tables. This is essential for computer science history because, on the Macintosh, if you are trying to do research on any kind of subject, the subject will pull up books, magazines, and links in one go. It was hypertext that would take a step further in building the internet.
